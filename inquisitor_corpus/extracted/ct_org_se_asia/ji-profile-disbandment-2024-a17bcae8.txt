[2]
Jemaah Islamiyah (JI) is an Islamist extremist organization based in Indonesia that seeks to establish an Islamic caliphate extending across Southeast Asia. The group originated in the late 1980s and was responsible for a number of high-profile attacks throughout the 1990s and early 2000s. These attacks mainly targeted Christians and Western interests, including the 2002 Bali nightclub bombings that killed over 200. Following increased counterterrorism efforts and internal splintering in the mid-2000s, JI has become less active.
Narrative
Jemaah Islamiyah (translated as “Islamic community”) is an Islamist extremist group in Indonesia that seeks to overthrow the Indonesian government and create a pan-Islamic state in Southeast Asia.
[3]
JI has ideological origins in the Darul Islam (DI) movement of the 1950s and 1960s, in which insurgents of West Javanese descent carried out a violent campaign with the goal of establishing an Islamic state in Indonesia.
[4]
The exact nature of JI’s relationship to DI is debated. The group has been characterized as an “offshoot” of DI motivated to organize either in response to government suppression of DI leaders or as a result of internal conflict within DI.
[5]
Experts disagree on the exact date that JI was founded as an organization. Estimates range from the late 1980s to the early 1990s, suggesting that the group coalesced over time before formalizing.
[6]
The group was founded by Yemeni-born Indonesian clerics Abdullah Sungkar and Abu Bakar Ba’asyir. Despite both being co-founders, Sungkar served as JI’s emir, or top leader, providing the group with inspiration and direction, while Ba’asyir was his second in command.
[7]
In 1972, Sungkar and Ba’asyir set up a
pesantren
, or Islamic boarding school, called Pesantren al Mukmin in the city of Solo on the Indonesian island of Java to promote jihadist teachings. In 1973, the school moved to the village of Ngruki and subsequently became known as Pondok Ngruki. In the years after its founding, graduates of Pondok Ngruki set up similar pesantren in locations throughout the country, creating a network of schools that served as recruiting bases for JI. Many of the militants involved in JI’s major terrorist attacks, including the 2002 Bali bombings, the 2003 Marriott bombings, and the 2004 Australian embassy bombings, had direct ties to several of the most prominent schools in the network.
[8]
In the late 1980s, Sungkar  and Ba’asyir fled to Malaysia to evade prison sentences for their ties to DI.
[9]
There, they sent fighters to join the Islamic insurgents fighting against the Soviet Union in Afghanistan.
[10]
While in Afghanistan, these fighters were exposed to different ideologies, including Wahhabism and Salafism, that would later influence the group’s political goals and violent tactics.
[11]
Additionally, the militants gained valuable military training and forged links with other militants fighting in Afghanistan. These connections laid the groundwork of JI’s ties Al Qaeda (AQ) and a broader network of Southeast Asian militant organizations.
[12]
In the early 1990s, Sungkar drew on the militants who had fought in Afghanistan to form the “backbone” of his new JI organization.
[13]
In 1996, a JI committee authored the “General Guide for the Struggle of Al-Jama’ah Al-Islamiyah,” which served as a reference manual for senior JI members. The manual asserted JI’s goals – to establish an Islamic Caliphate – and outlined the stages of organizational development necessary to achieve those goals. It also provided information about JI’s internal structures and methods of carrying out operations.
[14]
Throughout the 1990s, JI militants traveled to Afghanistan, the Philippines, and Indonesia.  Many of JI’s men trained in Afghanistan from the early- through mid-1990s. Some of these training camps were affiliated with Al Qaeda (AQ).
[15]
In addition to training, JI reportedly received resources and advising from AQ throughout its formation.
[16]
By the mid-1990s, Sungkar had established training camps in the Philippines. This helped to strengthen the relationship between JI and the Moro Islamic Liberation Front, an Islamic separatist organization based in the southern Philippines.
[17]
JI relocated to Indonesia after political and economic crises in 1998 forced Indonesian President Suharto to resign from office. Shortly thereafter, Sungkar died from natural causesBa’asyir subsequently took control of the group to the dismay of many of Sungkar’s Indonesian recruits, who saw him as weak and overly accommodating.
[18]
Under Ba’asyir’s leadership, splits began to develop within JI over the group’s strategy. Soon, two divergent factions emerged, including a “moderate majority” wing, which opposed violent action against innocent civilians, and a “radical minority” wing led by Hambali (a regional head of JI), which supported such action. Ba’asyir sided with the latter wing, thereby allowing Hambali to radicalize the organization and direct its activities. With Hambali serving as JI’s operational leader, Ba’asyir’s role became limited to providing the group with ideological and inspirational leadership.
[19]
Conflict erupted between Christians and Muslims in Indonesia in 1999 and 2000 during the country’s transition to democracy. JI fighters gained operational experience by attacking Christian churches and priests – particularly in West Java, Sumatra, and Lombok – in response to massacres of Muslims by Christians.
[20]
The group was responsible for a string of church bombings in Indonesia in December 2000 that killed eighteen, as well as a series of bombings in Manila in the same month that killed twenty-two.
[21]
In the early 2000s, JI began to focus its attacks on Western targets, a shift that was reflected in its actions and then publicly announced by Ba’asyir in 2002. In December 2001, Singapore officials foiled a JI plot to attack U.S., Israeli, British, and Australian diplomatic buildings in Singapore.
[22]
Subsequent attacks on public spaces like malls, hotels, and restaurants demonstrated that the organization also seemed to become more willing to accept collateral loss of Muslim life.
[23]
In October 2002, JI perpetrated its most notorious attack when it bombed two Bali nightclubs popular with foreign tourists, especially Australians, killing 202.
[24]
Governments in Singapore, Malaysia, and the Philippines actively pursued JI within their own borders. The Indonesian government, however, initially resisted pressure from the United States and other neighboring states to crack down on the group.
[25]
Indonesia largely refused to acknowledge the presence of a nation-wide Islamic terrorist threat and was unwilling to campaign against this threat in front of its Muslim-majority population that doubted JI’s existence.
[26]
Indonesia was also undergoing a number of political changes, corruption scandals, and other instances of communal violence at the time that consumed the government’s attention.
[27]
Following JI’s 2002 bombing in Bali, Indonesian authorities joined other governments in cracking down on the group. In response, some of JI’s leadership sought to abandon mass-casualty terrorism and distance itself from AQ’s calls to attack Western targets.
[28]
While JI’s ultimate goal of establishing an Islamic state across Southeast Asia endured, the group officially began to focus on education and religious outreach. This shift away from violence catalyzed the group’s fragmentation. Hard-line members set about forming violent splinter cells that went on to mount attacks that were often attributed to JI as a whole. Notably, between 2003 and 2009, a JI splinter cell led by Noordin Mohammed Top – later known as Al Qaeda in the Malay Archipelago or Tanzim Qaedat al-Jihad – carried out several attacks that did not follow typical JI practices or were not directly ordered by the JI leadership. It is unclear whether Top was directly connected to Al Qaeda (AQ), but he emulated the group and adopted the nom de guerre “Aiman” after one of AQ’s leaders.
[29]
Top’s cell was tied to the attacks of the JW Marriot Hotel in 2003 and the Australian Embassy in 2004, the 2005 Bali bombing, and the suicide bombings at two Western hotels in Jakarta in July 2009.
[30]
Top was killed in a shoot-out with Indonesian authorities in 2009.
[31]
By the mid-2000s, counterterrorism efforts had begun to seriously degrade JI’s operational capabilities. Between 2002 and 2013, governments in Southeast Asia – including Thailand, Malaysia, Singapore, and Indonesia – arrested over 400 people with potential ties to the group. These arrests included JI’s operational chief in 2003 and several senior officials, such as Para Wijayanto in 2019 and Aries Sumarsono (known as Zulkarnaen) in 2020. Security forces have killed a number of JI’s leaders as well, including the group’s senior bomb-maker in 2005, operational leader Noordin Mohammed Top in 2009, and senior leader Sanusi in November 2012.
[32]
These increased efforts to target JI members not only decimated organizational leadership but also forced the remaining leaders to rethink the group’s strategy, disagreements over which led the group to fragment through 2010.
[33]
After JI’s leaders seemed to set the group on a firm path to becoming a nonviolent organization, Ba’asyir left in 2008 to form a new organization, Jemaah Ansharut Tauhid (JAT).
[34]
In 2010, Ba’asyir was arrested and then convicted the following year for operating a militant training camp in Aceh, Indonesia, funded by JAT. He was sentenced to fifteen years in jail.
[35]
The last attack associated with JI came in July 2009, when suicide bombers attacked the JW Marriot and Ritz-Carlton in Jakarta, killing seven and injuring over fifty.
[36]
Shortly after, a JI faction led by Noordin Mohammed Top appeared to claim responsibility for the attack.
[37]
Since then, JI splinter groups and other Indonesian militant organizations, including those loyal to the Islamic State (IS), have been more active and received more media attention than JI.
[38]
In 2014, Ba’ayshir allegedly declared his support for tIS from prison.
[39]
According to some experts, JI is firmly anti-IS and remains linked to al-Qaeda. However, Australian authorities claim that JI has pledged allegiance to the IS movement.
[40]
Despite JI’s relative dormancy in past few years and the arrests and killings of many of its high-ranking leaders, most experts agree that JI remains a danger.
[41]
According to the Institute for Policy Analysis of Conflict (IPAC), a Jakarta-based think tank, Jemaah Islamiyah is not an “immediate threat,” but rather one with a “25-year-time frame for achieving an Islamic State.”
[42]
Similarly, Bilveer Singh of the Rajaratnam School of International Studies (RSIS) notes that JI has “adopted a strategic decision to lie low in line with the concept of I’dad,” which calls for “in-depth preparation to participate in a future jihad.”
[43]
Moreover, while JI has not undertaken a terrorist attack in recent years, the group never officially renounced violence and has continued to recruit new members, raise funds and, since 2010, rebuild its military wing.
[44]
Further contributing to concerns about the resurgence of JI is the potential return of Southeast Asian militants who fought in Iraq and Syria, which could provide JI with combat and weapons expertise and new leadership.
[45]

(unknown – 1999): Sungkar founded JI with Abu Bakar Ba’asyir sometime in the late 1980s or early 1990s. In 1972, Sungkar co-founded a religious boarding school with Ba’asyir in Java, known as Pondok Ngruki, which taught many students who later be associated with JI.
[54]
He also set up a religious school in Malaysia, known as Pondok Pesantren Luqmanul Hakiem, that was later confirmed to be a “JI institution.”

In 1978, Sungkar and Ba’asyir were arrested for their alleged ties to Darul Islam, an insurgent movement that aimed to create an Islamic state in Indonesia. They were released from prison in 1982 but fled to Malaysia in 1985 when the Indonesian government sought to arrest them again. While in Malaysia, Sungkar and Ba’asyir developed ties with other militants, particularly those fighting in Afghanistan against the Soviet Union.
[56]
Sungkar led JI training efforts throughout the 1990s. Until his death in 1999, Sungkar served as JI’s top leader, providing the group with inspiration and direction.

It is unclear whether Top was directly connected to Al Qaeda (AQ), but he emulated the group and adopted the nom de guerre “Aiman” after one of AQ’s leaders.

Name Changes
JI is also known to as Al Jama'ah Al Islamiyah, which is reportedly the name that JI members use to refer to the organization.
[78]
Resources
In addition to recruiting its members from personal contacts and religious study groups, JI also draws from a network of over fifty Islamic boarding schools that are sympathetic to JI’s goals. Imprisoned JI members have also reportedly had success recruiting other prisoners in jail.
[85]
Funding comes from a variety of sources including member contributions, legitimate business activities, criminal activity, and financers in Saudi Arabia, Yemen, and the broader Middle East.
[86]
There is also evidence of financial support from Al Qaeda (AQ), and some analysts believe that AQ funded the 2002 Bali bombing.
[87]
In 2018, Indonesia’s counterterrorism police, Densus 88, discovered that JI was using palm oil plantations on the islands of Sumatra and Borneo as a source of funding. According to a police spokesperson, JI used income from the plantations to fund its operations and pay salaries to its members.
[88]
JI has also raised funds through charitable organizations serving as fronts for the group. The non-governmental entity Hilal Ahmar Society Indonesia (HASI) is one such organization. Referred to by the United States as JI’s “humanitarian wing,” HASI provided funds that JI has used to recruit members and pay for the deployment of JI fighters to Syria. The U.S. Department of Treasury designated HASI as a Specially Designated Global Terrorist in September 2014.
[89]
In late 2020, following the arrests of JI leader Zulkarnaen and twenty-three other members, Indonesian police discovered a new charitable front for JI: the Abdurrachman bin Auf (ABA) Charity Foundation. According to police, JI had legally registered more than 20,000 donation boxes across 12 regions in Indonesia to ABA and placed them outside public places, including minimarkets, gas stations, restaurants, and shops. The money collected from the boxes has apparently been used to buy weapons and explosives and to fund training for JI members in Syria.
[90]

Ideology and Goals
Some analysts describe JI’s ideology as a combination of Darul Islam and Saudi Wahhabism.
[94]
JI’s “de facto” manifesto, the “General Guide for the Struggle of Al-Jama’ah Al‑Islamiyah” (known by its Indonesian acronym PUPJI), outlines the group’s primary objective, which includes the overthrow of the Indonesian government and the eventual creation of  a pan-Islamic state in Southeast Asia spanning across Indonesia, Malaysia, southern Thailand, Singapore, Brunei, and the southern Philippines. According to PUPJI, such an outcome is to be achieved by first cultivating a core group of leaders (Qiy’adah Rosyidah), who possess “true” faith, knowledge and leadership skills, and then building a solid base of followers (Qoi’dah Sholabah), who are obedient and dedicated to the group. The final stage of JI’s evolution includes the group’s transformation into a “‘secure’ base” from which the group could embark on a campaign of armed struggle, or
jihad
. While PUPJI does emphasize the importance of education and religious outreach, it considers engagement in violence as essential to realizing the group’s overarching objectives.
[95]
Political Activities
While JI has historically stayed out of the political realm, a report released in 2017 by the Institute for Policy Analysis of Conflict (IPAC) asserts that Para Wijayanto, JI’s emir from 2007 until his arrest in 2019, encouraged the group’s involvement in politics. Notably, under Wijayanto’s leadership, JI members participated in mass demonstrations against then-Jakarta Governor Basuki “Ahok” Tjahaja Purnama for making remarks about the Quran that hardline Muslims considered blasphemous. The IPAC report also noted that the younger generation of JI has expressed greater interest in political influence and political infiltration.
[96]
Targets And Tactics
In the late 1990s and early 2000s, most of JI’s operations targeted Christians. The group carried out attacks on churches and priests in response to massacres of Muslims by Christians, particularly in West Java, Sumatra, and Lombok.
[97]
In 2002, JI leader Ba’asyir publicly announced the organizational shift toward attacking Western interests. Common targets included diplomatic facilities and tourist destinations, such as hotels. The group primarily used bombs in attacks.
[98]
In the early to mid 2000s, facing increased security efforts by the Indonesian government, JI began to shift toward using nonviolent tactics, including preaching and religious outreach, in its efforts to create an Islamic caliphate in Southeast Asia. While the future operational trajectory of JI remains uncertain, terrorism and security experts note that JI’s shift away from violence does not necessarily indicate that the group intends to follow a nonviolent path. Rather, it may be that JI has made the strategic decision to focus on the jihadist concept of i’dad, which emphasizes “rebuilding in times of weakness in order to prepare for future Islamist assaults.”
[99]

Community Relations
The Indonesian public initially was largely unenthusiastic about opposing JI and other Islamist terrorism groups, in part because many Indonesians doubted the group’s existence in its early years.
[123]
After continued attacks – in particular the Bali Bombings in 2002 – public disapproval of terrorism perpetrated by Islamist militants increased in Indonesia. This culminated in the 2004 election of Susilo Bambang Yudhoyono to the Indonesian presidency, a general famous for his opposition to terrorism.
[124]
Relationships With Other Groups
JI has links to Al Qaeda (AQ), although reports vary on how strong these ties remain. Some analysts go so far as to say that JI was essentially an AQ branch in Southeast Asia.
[125]
Others claim the goals of the two organizations do not align, as JI focuses on establishing a regional caliphate while AQ ultimately aspires to have global influence.
[126]
AQ reportedly provided training, resources, and advising to JI leaders from its creation, beginning in the late 1980s.
[127]
Some members of JI received training in AQ affiliated camps in Afghanistan during the mid-1990s.
[128]
Multiple individuals from JI have either worked for or maintained close ties with both JI and AQ. For example, senior JI leader Hambali was reportedly AQ’s director of operations in East Asia.
[129]
Hambali had close ties to core AQ operatives and is believed to have been instrumental in coordinating the relationship between AQ and JI. Analysts also agree that AQ has influenced JI ideologically and encouraged JI members to carry out mass-casualty attacks on Western targets, like the 2002 Bali bombing.
[130]
According to terrorism analyst Sidney Jones, the years 1997–2002 “were the period of maximum communication and cooperation between JI and al-Qaeda.” Since then, it appears that AQ’s influence on the group has declined.
[131]
Importantly, however, as pointed out by international security analyst Dr. Peter Chalk, JI’s current leadership still contains members who were present when JI first established relations with al-Qaeda in Afghanistan. According to Chalk, “[t]he continued relevance of these ranking members in JI’s spine arguably signifies that the movement’s relationship with al-Qaeda is not only still very much alive but that those ties may well be on a far higher plane than previously assumed.”
[132]
In 2008, JI founder and spiritual leader Ba’aysir founded a new splinter group, Jemaah Ansharut Tauhid (JAT), after clashing with other JI leaders over the group’s strategy.
[133]
Despite disagreements at the leadership level, JAT has allegedly drawn a number of members from JI.
[134]
In 2014, the group suffered a split after Ba’asyir publicly pledged an oath of allegiance to IS’s leader, Abu Bakar al-Baghdadi. His decision was allegedly denounced by more than half of JAT’s members, including top officials and Ba’asyir’s sons, who subsequently left JAT to form a new rival organization called Jamaah Ansharusy Syariah (JAS), led by Mochammad Achwan.
[135]
JI has ties to other Islamist groups operating in Indonesia and broader Southeast Asia, especially those sharing origins in Darul Islam. These groups include Jamaah Anshurat Tauhid, Front Pembela Islam, Majelis Mujahidin Indonesia, KOMPAK, Lashkar Jundullah and Majelis Dakwah Umat Indonesia.
[136]
Its 1990s training camps in Mindanao helped establish a relationship with the Moro Islamic Liberation Front in the Philippines, and the groups have reportedly maintained good relations.
[137]
JI also reportedly has some ties to the Abu Sayyaf Group in the Philippines.
[138]
Although JI’s co-founder and former leader Ba’aysir allegedly announced support for the Islamic State (IS) in 2014, JI appears to have taken an anti-IS stance.
[139]
For example, Abu Tholut (also known as Imron), a former senior member of JI who was sentenced to eight years in prison in 2011 for involvement in terrorism, has openly denounced IS, calling it a “misguided organization” that has “lied to their people” and “spilt the blood of the innocent.”
[140]
Expert Bilveer Singh claims that most JI members “believe that ISIS is a proxy of the West and has to be countered as it has brought disrepute to jihad and the struggle of Muslims worldwide.”
[141]
State Sponsors And External Influences
As of June 2021, JI does not appear to have a state sponsor.
